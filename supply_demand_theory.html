1. Basic Concepts

What is Supply and Demand?

Supply and demand is the fundamental economic model explaining price determination in a market economy. It describes the relationship between the availability of a commodity (supply) and the desire for that commodity (demand).

  • Demand: The quantity of a good consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices
  • Supply: The quantity of a good producers are willing and able to offer at various prices
  • Market: Where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods and services

Key Assumptions

  1. Rational actors: Consumers maximize utility, producers maximize profit
  2. Perfect information: All participants know market conditions
  3. Free market: No external interference or monopolies
  4. Ceteris paribus: "All else equal" - we change one variable at a time

4. Market Equilibrium

Finding Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs where supply equals demand:

Qd = Qs
a - b Ã— P = c + d Ã— P

Solving for equilibrium price:
P* = (a - c) / (b + d)

Then equilibrium quantity:
Q* = a - b Ã— P*

Characteristics of Equilibrium

  1. Market clearing: No shortage or surplus
  2. Stable: Tendency to return if disturbed
  3. Efficient: Maximum total surplus achieved
  4. Dynamic: Changes as market conditions change

Disequilibrium Situations

Shortage (Excess Demand)

  • Price below equilibrium
  • Qd > Qs
  • Upward pressure on price
  • Example: Concert tickets priced too low

Surplus (Excess Supply)

  • Price above equilibrium
  • Qs > Qd
  • Downward pressure on price
  • Example: Overproduced seasonal goods

5. Shifts vs. Movements

Movement Along the Curve

  • Cause: Change in the good's own price
  • Effect: Move to different point on same curve
  • Example: Gas price rises, people buy less gas

Shift of the Entire Curve

  • Cause: Change in external factors (income, costs, preferences)
  • Effect: Entire curve moves left/right or up/down
  • Example: Income rises, demand for restaurant meals increases at all prices

6. Price Elasticity

Definition

Price elasticity measures how responsive quantity is to price changes:

Elasticity = % Change in Quantity / % Change in Price

Types of Elasticity

Demand Elasticity

  • Elastic (|E| > 1): Quantity very responsive to price
    • Luxury goods, substitutes available
    • Simulator: Flatter demand curve
  • Inelastic (|E| < 1): Quantity not very responsive to price
    • Necessities, no substitutes
    • Simulator: Steeper demand curve

Supply Elasticity

  • Elastic: Easy to increase/decrease production
    • Simulator: Steeper supply curve
  • Inelastic: Hard to change production quickly
    • Simulator: Flatter supply curve

Elasticity Toggle in Simulator

Normal Mode: Standard elasticity values

  • Demand slope = 0.8
  • Supply slope = 0.9

Elastic Mode: Higher price sensitivity

  • Demand slope = 0.4 (flatter, more responsive)
  • Supply slope = 1.8 (steeper, more responsive)

Consumer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay.

Producer Surplus

Producer surplus is the difference between what producers receive and their minimum acceptable price.

  • Graphically: Area above supply curve, below market price
  • Formula: Â½ Ã— (Equilibrium Price - Min Price) Ã— Equilibrium Quantity
  • Interpretation: Producer benefit from market participation

Total Surplus

Total surplus = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus

  • Measures total economic benefit
  • Maximized at equilibrium
  • Reduces with price controls or market inefficiencies

9. Real-World Applications

Market Examples

Elastic Demand

  • Luxury cars: Price increase â†’ large decrease in sales
  • Restaurant dining: Many substitutes available
  • Vacation travel: Postponable, discretionary spending

Inelastic Demand

  • Gasoline: Few substitutes for transportation
  • Medical care: Necessity regardless of price
  • Basic food items: Essential for survival

Elastic Supply

  • Digital products: Easy to scale production
  • Service industries: Can hire more workers quickly
  • Manufacturing with excess capacity

Inelastic Supply

  • Agricultural products: Fixed growing seasons
  • Real estate: Limited land availability
  • Specialized skilled labor: Takes time to train

Market Events in Reality

Supply Shocks

  • Oil crisis (1973): Reduced supply â†’ higher prices
  • COVID-19: Supply chain disruptions
  • Natural disasters: Affect production capacity

Demand Shocks

  • Housing bubble (2008): Speculation increased demand
  • Pandemic lockdowns: Reduced demand for services
  • Technology adoption: Smartphones increased demand for data

Policy Implications

Price Controls

  • Price ceiling: Creates shortages if below equilibrium
  • Price floor: Creates surpluses if above equilibrium
  • Examples: Rent control, minimum wage

Market Interventions

  • Taxes: Shift supply curve up/left
  • Subsidies: Shift supply curve down/right
  • Regulations: Affect production costs and supply

Key Takeaways

For Students

  1. Understand the basics: Supply up-sloping, demand down-sloping
  2. Differentiate shifts vs. movements: Internal price changes vs. external factors
  3. Recognize elasticity: How responsive markets are to price changes
  4. Apply to real situations: Connect theory to current events and personal experiences

For the Simulator

  1. Experiment freely: Try extreme values to see relationships
  2. Watch the surplus: Understand how efficiency changes
  3. Use random events: Experience market unpredictability
  4. Compare elasticity modes: See how market structure affects outcomes

Advanced Concepts to Explore

  • Cross-price elasticity: How related goods affect demand
  • Income elasticity: How income changes affect different goods
  • Long-run vs. short-run: How time affects elasticity
  • Market structures: Perfect competition vs. monopoly effects

This simulator provides a foundation for understanding microeconomic principles. Real markets are more complex, involving multiple goods, imperfect information, and various market structures, but the fundamental supply and demand framework remains essential for economic analysis.
